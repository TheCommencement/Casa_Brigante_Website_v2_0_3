On keeping a catalog.

The house was asked recently why nothing is ever retired. Why the first novel stands on the same shelf as the newest one. Why nothing goes on sale, nothing gets a farewell, and nothing quietly disappears when a better version of the author shows up to be embarrassed by it.

The answer begins with a shelf.

A shelf does not apologize for what it holds. It does not rank its own contents or hide the spines that sold slowly. A shelf keeps things in the order they arrived, and in doing so it tells the truth about the person who filled it. Take one book away and the shelf starts lying.

Most catalogs are managed like inventory. Titles get retired the way milk gets pulled: quietly, by the date. The work is treated as stock, and stock exists to move. When it stops moving, it stops existing.

Casa Brigantè was built on the opposite bet. The work is not stock. The work is the record. Every title, every recording, every episode is a dated entry in a ledger that says: this is what was made here, in this order, by these hands. The early work is not a liability to the later work. It is the proof that the later work was earned.

This costs something. Keeping everything in print means the clumsy sentence from years ago stays purchasable next to the sharpened one from last winter. So be it. An institution that edits its own history is not an institution. It is a marketing department with a memory problem.

So the catalog only grows. That is the whole policy, and it fits in four words. What the house makes, the house keeps, and anyone who walks in years from now will be able to run a finger along the shelf and read the entire story in the spines: where it began, where it stumbled, where it found its voice, and where it was going all along.

Filed from the desk of the founder.
